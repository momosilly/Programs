A Note on Roasting

When coffee beans are roasted, chemical reactions like caramelization and the Maillard reaction occur, creating the flavors and aromas that make coffee so distinctive.

Green Beans

22C°(72F°)

Green coffee is made from the raw, unroasted seeds of the coffee fruit. Unlike the familiar brown beans, these are simply dried and processed without undergoing roasting. Because they haven’t been roasted, green coffee beans retain more of their natural compounds—especially chlorogenic acid, which tends to decrease during roasting. Although it can be brewed, green coffee has a sharp, grassy flavor and is more often used in health products as an extract due to its potential wellness benefits

Light Roast

205C°(401F°)

Light roast coffee is made by roasting beans at lower temperatures for a shorter time. This gentle process produces pale brown beans with a dry, matte surface and helps preserve the natural qualities of the original coffee. The result is a brew with lively, intricate flavors—often floral or fruity—with noticeable acidity and a clean, tea-like texture. Unlike darker roasts, light roasts don’t carry strong, smoky or chocolatey notes.

City Roast

219C°(426F°)

City roast is a light-to-medium roast level where beans are heated until the first audible crack finishes, but before the second one begins. This careful timing results in dry, pale brown beans that preserve the bean’s natural acidity and showcase vibrant, fruity notes. With a lighter body and a clean finish, city roasts are ideal for brewing methods like pour-over, which bring out the subtle flavors tied to the bean’s origin

Vienna Roast

230C°(446F°)

Vienna roast is a medium-dark roast that sits between a standard medium roast and the deeper French roast. The beans are dark brown with a slight sheen of oil, and the flavor is smooth and well-rounded, often featuring hints of chocolate, toasted nuts, and a touch of caramel sweetness. Roasted just as the second crack begins, this level produces a rich, full-bodied cup with lower acidity and a subtle smoky undertone. Its balanced profile makes it a great fit for a variety of coffee styles

Italian Roast

245C°(473F°)

Italian roast takes coffee beans to one of the darkest roast levels, even deeper than French roast. The intense heat gives the beans a nearly black appearance with a glossy, oily surface. This roast produces a bold, full-bodied flavor with smoky undertones and a slightly burnt edge, offering a rich taste that’s both strong and layered.

Brazil


Coffee from Brazil—recognized as the top global producer—is known for its mellow taste, low acidity, and flavor notes that often include chocolate and nuts. These beans typically offer a smooth, creamy body with a touch of sweetness, making them a popular choice in espresso blends. Brazil cultivates both Arabica and Robusta varieties, though premium coffees often showcase the refined qualities of Arabica beans.

India


Coffee cultivated in India has a long-standing heritage dating back to the 1600s. Known for its full-bodied character and low acidity, Indian coffee often carries earthy and spicy undertones—flavors reminiscent of spices like cardamom and nutmeg. One of its most distinctive processing techniques is Monsoon Malabar, which exposes beans to monsoon winds, giving them a mellow, aged profile. Grown primarily in southern states such as Karnataka, Kerala, and Tamil Nadu, India produces both Arabica and Robusta beans, along with specialty types like Mysore Nuggets and Peaberry, offering a rich and layered experience for coffee enthusiasts around the world.

Ethiopa


Ethiopia, often considered the birthplace of coffee, produces beans known for their vibrant and layered flavor profiles. Depending on the region and how the beans are processed, the taste can vary widely—from fruity and wine-like notes in naturally dried beans to delicate floral hints like jasmine and lemongrass in washed varieties. Regions such as Yirgacheffe, Sidama, and Harrar are especially famous for their distinctive coffees, which are commonly featured in premium single-origin offerings and play a central role in Ethiopia—s traditional coffee ceremonies.

Costa Rica


Costa Rica produces premium Arabica coffee, especially in its elevated volcanic areas, where the beans develop vibrant acidity, natural sweetness, and flavor notes like citrus, apricot, and chocolate. Each growing region offers its own unique taste, with Tarrazu standing out as one of the most celebrated. The country is also known for its innovative techniques, such as the honey processing method, and maintains high standards in cultivation, including strict environmental practices around water use and waste management.

Kenya


Kenya produces some of the world’s most distinguished Arabica coffee, grown in nutrient-rich volcanic soil at high elevations. Known for its lively acidity and layered flavors—often with hints of citrus, berries, and a wine-like brightness—Kenyan coffee has been cultivated since the late 19th century. Today, it’s primarily grown by small-scale farmers working through cooperatives, alongside larger estates. The beans are typically washed and carefully sorted by size using a grading system, with top-tier grades like AA and PB reserved for export.
